## Chapter 4

### Indicator 4.1: Patent application issues
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** Patents Act No.2 of 2003
- **Evidence/Impact:** Part 4 of the Act prescribes the application for patents and Part 5 prescribes procedure for granting patent. A person shall filling an application with the Registrar of Patents (Section 12). If the Registrar determines the application does not comply with this Act, the Registrar must inform the applicant and ask to amend the application (Section 16). There is no restrictions related to the application process for patents.
- **Timeframe:** Assented July 2003, commencement February 2011
- **References:** https://wipolex.wipo.int/en/text/475502

### Indicator 4.2: Patent enforcement issues: civil and administrative procedures and remedies, provisional and other measures
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** Patents Act No.2 2003
- **Evidence/Impact:** Patent enforcement involves both civil and administrative procedures. The patent owner can initiate court proceedings for any alleged infringement (Section 42.1), including an injunction restraining the defendant from any alleged act of infringement (Section 42.2a). Administratively, the Registrar manages the submission, examination, and publication of patent applications, resolves disputes between applicants (Section 21), and has the authority to revoke patents on specific grounds (Section 39). Remedies include injunctions, damages, destruction of infringing products, and declarations of validity and infringement.
- **Timeframe:** Assented July 2003, commencement February 2011
- **References:** http://www.paclii.org/cgi-bin/sinodisp/vu/legis/num_act/pa2003109/pa2003109.html?stem=&synonyms=&query=patent

### Indicator 4.2: Patent enforcement issues: civil and administrative procedures and remedies, provisional and other measures
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** Patents Act No.2 of 2003
- **Evidence/Impact:** According to Part 11 of the Act, patent infringement and proceedings are regulated. If a person infringes a patent invention, the owner of a patent may bring proceedings in court for any act alleged to infringe the patent (Section 41). The remedies cover an injunction, damages for the infringement, an account of the profits derived by the defendant from infringement, etc. (Section 42).

There is no restriction or report related to the enforcement of patents.
- **Timeframe:** Assented July 2003, commencement February 2011

### Indicator 4.4: Lack of copyright framework and exceptions

### Indicator 4.5: Online copyright enforcement issues: civil and administrative procedures and remedies; and provisional measures

### Indicator 4.8: Mandatory disclosure of trade secrets, such as source code and algorithms
- **Score:** 0

### Indicator 4.9: Lack of effective trade secrets legal framework
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** Trade Secrets Act of 2000 No.52 of 2000
- **Evidence/Impact:** The Trade Secrets Act defines a trade secret as any information that is (Section 1):
(a) not generally known among, or readily accessible to, persons who
normally deal with information of that kind; or
(b) about a person’s business or professional affairs and disclosure would, or could reasonably be expected to, unreasonably affect that person adversely in respect of his or her lawful business or professional affairs;  and that has commercial value because of this. 

If a trade secret is acquired, used or disclosed by a person without the authority of the owner of the trade secret, the owner has the right to apply to the Supreme Court for a declaration that the trade secret has been acquired, used or disclosed without authority.  The Court may: grant an injunction preventing anyone from dealing with, or otherwise using, the secret in a commercial way; and if it is satisfied that the owner has suffered damage because of the unauthorised disclosure—award damages to the owner (Section 3).
- **Timeframe:** Assented December 2000, commencement February 2011
- **References:** https://www.wto.org/english/thewto_e/acc_e/vut_e/wtaccvut14_leg_30.pdf

### Indicator 7.1: Lack of comprehensive legal framework for data protection
- **Score:** 0.5
- **Source Legislation:** Electronic Transaction Act No.24 of 2000;

Cybercrime Act No.22 of 2021;

Telecommunications and Radiocommunications Regulation Act No. 30 of 2009
- **Evidence/Impact:** Comprehensive data protection laws are currently before parliament. Data protection laws can be found in sectoral laws, such as the Electronic Transaction Act (Section 25), the Cybercrime Act, and Telecommunications and Radiocommunications Regulation Act (Section 40). 

Specifically, the Telecommunications Act provides that a service provider must not, without the consent of the end user, divulge any personal end user information to any other person who is not an agent or employee of the service provider, unless required by law or authorised by warrant or by the Regulator (Section 40(1)). A service provider must not, without the consent of the end user, collect any personal end user information not reasonably necessary for the provision of any telecommunications service to the end user, unless required by law or authorised by warrant or by the Regulator (Section 40(2)).
