### Indicator 3.4: Screening of investment and acquisitions
Exception: Anti-trust measures related to M&A are not considered a restriction, unless discriminatory.
- **Score:** 0.25
- **Source Legislation:** Foreign Exchange and Foreign Trade Act (Act No. 228 of 1949);

Foreign Exchange and Foreign Trade Act, Foreign Investment Screening System, Annual Report (FY2023)
- **Evidence/Impact:** Japan's inward direct investment screening system started with the Foreign Capital Act enacted in 1950 as a special law for the then (Foreign Exchange and Foreign Trade Act) FEFTA.  Please refer to page 7 of the FEFTA Foreign Investment Screening System Annual Report (FY2023). 

Under the FEFTA, the Paragraphs (3) through (5), Article 27 of FEFTA stipulate a series of process before the ministries finally determine to issue the orders for modification or discontinuance of inward direct investments. Please refer to the relevant paragraphs of FEFTA. Specifically, Article 27, Paragraph (3), (i) states that the Minister of Finance of Japan is responsible for evaluating the impact of an investment on the preservation of production foundations and technologies within specific business sectors. If the investment poses (a) a threat to national security, disrupts the maintenance of public order, or hinders public safety, the Minister has the authority to reject the foreign investment, and (b) has Significant adverse effect to the Japanese economy.

Certain business sectors requiring prior notification of inward direct investment are defined as ‘designated business sectors’, including business sectors that could significantly undermine national security, which are classified as ‘core business sectors’. Core business sectors, a type of business involving inward direct investments that are likely to undermine Japan’s national security, includes semi-conductors; certain cybersecurity-related business (such as network security monitoring, service providers of the programmes designed for critical infrastructures); and certain infrastructure-related business (such as electricity, gas, telecommunications, water supply, railway and oil).

Foreign investors who plan to acquire 1% or more of stocks and shares of Japanese companies engaging in designated business sectors are required to submit prior notifications for screening from the viewpoints of Japan’s national security and other objectives of FEFTA. (When digital trade in which investee Japanese companies engage fall into the designated business sectors, foreign investors are required to submit the prior notifications.) The Ministry of Finance and other ministries responsible for relevant business sectors screen the notifications to see if such investments impair Japan’s national security or other objectives under FEFTA. If the screening finds it does, the authorities may recommend or order the modification or discontinuance of the investments after hearing opinions from the Council on Customs, Tariff, Foreign Exchange and Other Transactions. If the screening finds it doesn’t, foreign investors are allowed to undertake investments after screening period.

For business in core business sectors, foreign investors cannot attend meetings of the target company’s board of directors or other committees that make important decisions on such business. Moreover, foreign investors cannot, by themselves or through their designated person, make proposals, in writing, to (a) the target company’s board of directors or other committees that make important decisions or (b) the members of such board or committees, requiring responses or actions by certain deadlines.

### Indicator 4.2: Patent enforcement issues: civil and administrative procedures and remedies, provisional and other measures
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** Patent Act (Act No. 121 of 1959);

Civil Provisional Remedies Act（Act No. 91 of 1989）
- **Evidence/Impact:** Under Japan's Patent Act (Articles 100–106), patent holders can pursue civil remedies to address infringement. These remedies include the right to seek injunctions to halt infringing activities and claim compensation for damages. Damages may be calculated based on the patent holder’s lost profits or the infringer’s profits derived from the infringement. Additionally, under Article 100 (Right to demand an Injunction), the Patent Act allows courts to order corrective measures, such as the disposal of products constituting the act of infringement, ensuring the cessation of unlawful activities. If the patented invention is a process by which a product is produced, this includes articles produced through infringement; the same applies in Article 102, paragraph (1)) and the removal of equipment used to infringe.

Japan ensures patent holders can protect their rights through provisional measures under the Civil Provisional Remedies Act. Article 13 allows courts to issue preliminary injunctions to stop infringement and preserve evidence, provided the applicant demonstrates the need for protection.

- **Score:** 0

Article 119 of Copyright Act imposes penalties for unauthorized downloads and copyright infringement, including digital content, ensuring deterrence against illegal activity. Article 30 defines exceptions for personal use, permitting lawful reproduction within limits while excluding minor infringements, such as screenshots, from penalties. 

Japan ensures copyright holders can protect their rights through provisional measures under the Civil Provisional Remedies Act. Article 13 allows courts to issue preliminary injunctions to stop infringement and preserve evidence, provided the applicant demonstrates the existence of the copyright and the necessity for protection.

### Indicator 4.9: Lack of effective trade secrets legal framework
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** Unfair Competition Prevention Act (Act No. 47 of 1993);

Management Guidelines for
Trade Secrets
- **Evidence/Impact:** The Unfair Competition Prevention Act regulates infringement of trade secrets, unfair usage of a well-known sign, misleading representation regarding the place of origin, imitation of the configuration of a third party’s product, etc. The Unfair Competition Prevention Act provides civil and criminal remedies in such cases where secret information of the company is stolen or disclosed illegally. To protect information according to the Act, companies need to manage such data as “trade secrets”.

Moreover, the Management Guidelines for Trade Secrets, established by the Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry, specify a set of concepts that it has from the viewpoint of an administrative organ in charge of the Unfair Competition Prevention Act (hereinafter, “the Act”), and of managing trade agreements including Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights (TRIPS). The Guidelines address the definitions and other details of trade secrets including requirements for protection under the Unfair Competition Prevention Act, based on the goal of promoting innovation, overseas trends and typical court decisions both at home and abroad (although no decisions have yet been given by the Supreme Court of Japan on these issues as of the current revision of the Guidelines), along with other aspects. The Guidelines, however, are not legally binding.
