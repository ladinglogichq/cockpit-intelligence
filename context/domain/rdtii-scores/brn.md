### Indicator 2.3: Limitations in procurement bidding
- **Score:** 0.5
- **Source Legislation:** Local Business Development (LBD) Policy Framework for the ICT Industry in Brunei Darussalam 2017;

Local Business Development Circular Ministry of Development Issue No.1/2019 2019
- **Evidence/Impact:** While there is no ban on foreign participation in public procurement based on nationality or status, there are several measures that restrict that participation, such as policies favoring local businesses or joint ventures with local firms or the LBD policy that mandates foreign ICT companies to meet local content requirements, such as employing local workers. 

The LBD Circular (Section 6.1.3) specifies the need to meet local employment targets in government projects. It mandates that project owners should design projects that attract local employment and ensure that companies comply with local employment requirements as per the project targets outlined in Paragraph 3. This paragraph sets different levels of local employment targets based on the class of the company and the size of the project. For instance, Class I requires 100% local employment whereas Class VI requires a minimum of 50% local employment. 

Section 3.1 of the LBD Circular outlines the local content targets that contractors must achieve. These targets vary by the class of the contracting company and the value of the project, ranging from 30% to 100% local content for larger projects . It is also mandated that project owners must submit a Contractor Scoring Assessment Report assessing contractors’ compliance with LBD requirements. This is to be submitted to the Authority Building Control and Construction Industry (ABCi) during the project. Section 6.3.2 prioritizes local companies in the tender recommendation process.

### Indicator 3.4: Screening of investment and acquisitions
Exception: Anti-trust measures related to M&A are not considered a restriction, unless discriminatory.
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** Authority for Info-communications Technology Industry of Brunei Darussalam (AITI) Order No.S39 2001;

Petroleum Mining Act CAP.44 1963 Revised Edition 2002;

Banking Order No.S45/06 2006;

Islamic Banking Order No.S96/08 2008
- **Evidence/Impact:** There have been no documented instances in Brunei Darussalam where a direct foreign investment was blocked through the use of a formal screening mechanism. Brunei Darussalam's approach to FDI has largely been to encourage investment in line with its economic diversification goals only with the exception of strategic sectors such as telecommunications. The government has identified 5 key sectors among which we can find ICT, so they're promoting FDI in that sector.

Brunei Darussalam does not have a specific, standalone Foreign Investment Screening Law. However, foreign investment in Brunei Darussalam is regulated through a combination of general laws, policies, and sector-specific regulations that address the entry and establishment of foreign investments. Foreign investment screening may be embedded in sector-specific regulations, particularly in strategic industries such as energy, telecommunications, and finance. These sectors often have additional rules or approvals needed for foreign investment to ensure national interests are protected.

Foreign investment in Brunei Darussalam's telecommunications, energy, and financial services sectors is tightly regulated. In telecommunications, under the AITI Order, 2001, foreign investors need licenses like InTi and SeTi for providing services, and must secure Type Approvals for importing telecom equipment. The oil and gas sector is governed by the Oil Mining Act (Cap 44), where foreign companies often need joint ventures with Petroleum Brunei due to strategic importance. In financial services, regulated by the Banking Order, 2006 and Islamic Banking Order, 2008, foreign financial institutions must obtain licenses from AMBD, and may face ownership restrictions or be required to follow Shariah law for Islamic banking.

- **Evidence/Impact:** In Brunei Darussalam, the licensing requirements for online payment services are primarily governed by the "Notice for Payment System Operators," issued by the Autoriti Monetari Brunei Darussalam (AMBD). A key requirement is the need for operators to obtain prior approval from the BDCB before commencing any payment system activities. Specifically, Section 4 mandates that no person shall operate a payment system without this approval. To apply, entities must submit detailed particulars and additional information as requested by the Authority, along with a non-refundable application fee. This structured approach ensures that the AMBD can adequately assess the suitability and viability of the payment systems before they are launched.

Another critical aspect of the licensing process is the fit and proper criteria outlined in Section 7 of the Notice. Applicants must demonstrate that their directors, chief executive officers, shareholders, and employees meet standards of integrity, competence, and financial soundness. Additionally, the applicant must be a company incorporated under the Companies Act and maintain a permanent place of business in Brunei. This requirement ensures that only reputable and capable entities can operate payment systems, thus promoting stability and trust in the financial ecosystem.

Financial requirements further underpin the licensing framework, including a minimum base capital of B$100,000 and a security deposit of B$50,000 to be maintained with the Authority. Section 8 emphasizes that operators must not only maintain adequate capital buffers but also keep comprehensive records of transactions and appoint an auditor for annual audits. These stipulations are crucial for ensuring ongoing compliance, accountability, and transparency within the payment services sector, ultimately protecting consumers and enhancing the overall security of financial transactions in Brunei.

Moreover, payment service providers in Brunei Darussalam are restricted from engaging in activities that fall outside the scope of their licensed operations, as outlined in Section 5 of the Notice for Payment System Operators. Specifically, Section 5.1 states that any person operating a payment system must obtain the Authority's approval for their payment system, and Section 5.2 mandates that an operator can only conduct the business of providing payment services for which it is licensed and must not engage in any other activities or businesses unless permitted by the Authority. This ensures that operators remain focused on their designated services and comply with relevant regulations, preventing them from engaging in activities such as lending or investment services without obtaining the necessary additional licenses​.

Overall, while Brunei Darussalam's licensing requirements are aligned with global norms aimed at ensuring financial stability and consumer protection, they may be more rigid in structure compared to the more flexible and innovation-friendly frameworks in countries like Singapore and the United Kingdom.
