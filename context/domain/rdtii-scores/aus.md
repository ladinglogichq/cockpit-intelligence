- **Score:** 0.5

Commonwealth Procurement Rules 2024;

Telecommunications Act 1997;

- **Evidence/Impact:** In general, Australia does not exclude foreign firms from public procurement. At the federal level, officials from corporate and non-corporate Commonwealth entities listed in the Public Governance, Performance, and Accountability (PGPA) Rule 2014 must comply with the Commonwealth Procurement Rules (CPRs) (CPRs 2.1-2.2). CPRs contain a non-discrimination principle, which provides that " All potential suppliers to government must, subject to these CPRs, be treated equitably based on their commercial, legal, technical and financial abilities and not be discriminated
against due to their size, degree of foreign affiliation or ownership, location, or the origin of
their goods and services." (CPRs 5.4).

However, the Australian Government retains the discretion to exclude foreign firms from participating in critical national infrastructure. This power has been exercised in the past for national security reasons, as seen with the 2012 ban on Huawei from the National Broadband Network (NBN) rollout. Similarly, in 2018, both Huawei and ZTE were banned from Australia’s 5G rollout on similar grounds. In 2023, TikTok was also banned from federal government devices due to security concerns under the Protective Security Policy Framework.

### Indicator 4.9: Lack of effective trade secrets legal framework
- **Score:** 0.5
- **Source Legislation:** Corporations Act 2001;

Freedom of Information Act 1982;

Competition and Consumer Act 2010
- **Evidence/Impact:** Under Australian law, a 'trade secret' is considered to be merely a form of confidential information and does not have a clear or rigid meaning.

While Australia does not have a specific statutory regime for protecting trade secrets, there are various Acts which protect against the disclosure of confidential information through imposing an obligation of confidence. 

The Corporations Act 2001 provides that a person who obtains information because they are, or have been, a director or other officer or employee of a corporation must not improperly use the information.

Similarly, the Competition and Consumer Act 2010 states that a bargain between two parties never requires disclosure of a trade secret. 
In addition to claims for breach of contractual and statutory obligations of confidence, a common method of enforcing trade secrets that have been unlawfully disclosed is through the bringing of an equitable action for breach of confidence.

In addition, Australia complies with its obligations under article 39 of the 1995 Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights Agreement (TRIPS) by giving trade secrets legal protection through the equitable doctrine of "breach of confidence" and, where an appropriate clause exists, breach of contract.
- **Timeframe:** Since June 2001, last amended in October 2024;

Since March 1982, last amended in October 2024;

Since August 2010, last amended in October 2024
- **References:** https://www.legislation.gov.au/C2004A00818 | https://www.legislation.gov.au/C2004A00109 | https://www.legislation.gov.au/C2004A02562

### Indicator 5.1: Absence of passive infrastructure sharing
- **Score:** 0.5
- **Source Legislation:** Telecommunications Act 1997
- **Evidence/Impact:** Telecommunications carriers have statutory rights of access to other carriers' towers and ducts, but such access is not mandated. Passive infrastructure sharing is practiced in Australia in the mobile sector: co-location is negotiated on a commercial basis. Carriers have regulated rights of access to towers owned by other carriers for the purposes of providing carriage services. The Telecommunications Act 1997 provides for carriers to provide other carriers with access to telecommunications transmission towers, the sites of telecommunications transmission towers and eligible underground facilities. The Australian Competition and Consumer Commission has made a code which sets out conditions that are to be complied with in relation to the provision of access.

Passive infrastructure sharing is also practiced in Australia in the fixed sector: the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission has declared the following services: - line sharing service (LSS) - local carriage service (LCS) - fixed originating access service (FOAS) - fixed terminating access service (FTAS) - unconditioned local loop service (ULLS) - wholesale line rental (WLR) - wholesale ADSL service (WADSL) Once a service is declared, a network owner must provide access to the service upon request.

### Indicator 11.4: Deviation from international encryption standards (ISO, IEC, ITU, FIPS, AES, TDES, and ECC)
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** Intelligence Services Act 2001;

- **Evidence/Impact:** Under the Intelligence Services Act 2001, Australian Signals Directorate (ASD) conducts Cryptographic Evaluation. This evaluation regime was implemented in July 2019. Products interacting with Australian government systems and critical security environments, including the handling of classified data must use ASD Approved Cryptographic Algorithms and Protocols, in line with international standards.

ASD conducts "a combination of open source and in-house tests to ensure the correct implementation of cryptographic algorithms and protocols as well as assessing the quality of the surrounding cryptographic architecture." ASD Approved Cryptographic Algorithms are mostly internationally recognized or approved by National Institute of Standards and Technology. Such algorithms fall into three categories: asymmetric/public key algorithms, hashing algorithms, and symmetric encryption algorithms. 

The approved asymmetric/public key algorithms are: Diffie-Hellman (DH) for agreeing on encryption session keys, Digital Signature Algorithm (DSA) for digital signatures, Elliptic Curve Diffie-Hellman (ECDH) for key exchange, Elliptic Curve Digital Signature Algorithm (ECDSA) for digital signatures, and Rivest-Shamir-Adleman (RSA) for digital signatures and passing encryption session keys or similar keys.
The approved hashing algorithm is Secure Hashing Algorithm 2 (SHA-2) (i.e. SHA-224, SHA-256, SHA-384 and SHA-512).
The approved symmetric encryption algorithms are Advanced Encryption Standard (AES) using key lengths of 128, 192 and 256 bits.
