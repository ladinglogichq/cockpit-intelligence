### Indicator 4.1: Patent application issues
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** Patents Act 1994;

Patent (Amendment) Act 2017
- **Evidence/Impact:** No restriction on patent application process. The procedure is adhered to the Patent Cooperation Treaty (Sections 85-88). A foreign patent applicant can file an international patent application under the PCT, designating Singapore as one of the countries where they seek protection. The applicant must file a national phase application in Singapore within 30 months of the initial filing date of the PCT application if they wish to proceed with seeking patent protection in Singapore. This period could be extended by up to 18 months with a request and fee for extension.. The applicant can also file a patent online. 

Requirements/restrictions on the patent application process in Singapore include:
• Requirement to have an address for service in Singapore (Section 25 of the Patents Act read with Rules 19 and 31 of the Patents Rules)
• Restriction on applications outside Singapore by a Singapore resident unless with written authority, or an application has been filed in Singapore at least 2 months before filing outside Singapore (Section 34 of the Patents Act) 
Except for the restriction on applications outside Singapore by a Singapore resident, the other requirements/restrictions are applicable to all applications, regardless of whether they were filed by local or foreign individuals/entities.

### Indicator 4.9: Lack of effective trade secrets legal framework
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** Trade Secrets Enterprise Guide issued by Intellectual Property Office of Singapore;

Common Law doctrine of breach of confidence
- **Evidence/Impact:** In Singapore, there is no express legislation for the protection of trade secrets per se. Instead, protection of trade secrets in Singapore is typically through the common law doctrine of breach of confidence, as well as (where applicable) the use of IP and contract law. It protects all types of confidential information, such as your personal/private information (e.g., medical reports, photographs, receipts).

For information to be protectable as confidential information or a trade secret, it must not already be available to the public at large and it must be clearly and specifically identified (Nanofilm Technologies International Pte Ltd v Semivac International Pte Ltd and others [2018] SGHC 167). 

Additionally, the Intellectual Property Office of Singapore's Trade Secrets Enterprise Guide reports: In considering whether a breach of confidence is made out, the following will be considered: 1) whether the information has the quality of confidentiality; 2) whether the information was imparted in circumstances importing an obligation of confidentiality. An obligation of confidentiality can be found even where confidential information has been accessed or acquired without the company’s knowledge or consent; 3) whether the person who came into the information is able to demonstrate that his conscience was unaffected.  For instance that he was not aware that the information was confidential or he came across the information by accident.

Confidential information is protected by the common law doctrine – law of confidence, as well as contract law and various legislation(s) dependent on the circumstance. 

• For example, section 3 of the Computer Misuse Act 1993 may apply in cases where a person breaches cybersecurity in order to gain access to trade secrets whilst section 24 of the Personal Data Protection Act obligates the organisation collecting personal data to protect the personal data by making reasonable security arrangements. 
The Trade Secrets Enterprise Guide issued by the Intellectual Property Office of Singapore provides further guidance on this.

### Indicator 5.4: Lack of functional/accounting separation
- **Score:** 0.5
- **Source Legislation:** Telecommunications Act 1999;

Code of Practice for Competition in the Provision of Telecommunication and Media Services 2022 (Converged Code)  issued by Infocomm Media Development Authority (IMDA)
- **Evidence/Impact:** Accounting separation is required for the application of dominant licensees and their related companies. Under section 2.3 of the Converged Code, a licensee will be classified as ‘dominant’ if it is licensed to operate facilities that are sufficiently costly or difficult to replicate such that requiring new entrants to do so would create a significant barrier to rapid and successful entry into the telecommunication and (or) media market in Singapore by an efficient competitor; or if it has the ability to exercise significant market power in any market in Singapore in which it provides telecommunication or broadcasting services. 

The Telecom Competition Code Section 8.1.4.1.3 requires these operators to separate their financial accounts for different services to prevent cross-subsidization and to enhance cost transparency. Accounting separation involves the preparation of separate financial statements for different segments of the business, such as wholesale and retail operations.
