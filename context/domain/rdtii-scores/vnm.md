### Indicator 2.3: Limitations in procurement bidding
- **Score:** 1
- **Source Legislation:** Law No. 22/2023/QH15 on Bidding (Luật số 22/2023/QH15 của Quốc hội: Luật Đấu thầu)
- **Evidence/Impact:** Viet Nam’s new Law No. 22/2023/QH15  on Bidding, which has replaced Law No. 43/2013/QH13, continues to enforce preferences for domestic suppliers of goods and services as well as domestically made products. This preference is explicitly outlined in Article 10(1) of the new law, which grants preferential treatment to goods originating from Vietnam and to domestic contractors who produce such goods. This regulation significantly limits opportunities for foreign firms in the bidding process, as it provides a clear advantage to domestic suppliers in public procurement activities.

Moreover, Article 5(1)(i) of the new law maintains the requirement that foreign contractors must enter into partnerships with domestic contractors or employ domestic subcontractors when participating in international bidding in Vietnam. The only exception to this requirement is if no domestic contractor is qualified to participate in the specific public procurement package. This provision ensures that domestic entities are actively involved in major projects, further bolstering the local economy at the expense of limiting direct foreign competition.

Additionally, Article 10(1)(d) of the new law specifies that foreign contractors can benefit from preferential treatment if they partner with domestic contractors who undertake 25% or more of the work value of the bidding package. This clause provides a structured incentive for foreign contractors to collaborate with local companies, thereby reinforcing the emphasis on domestic participation and the development of local expertise and capacity in major projects.

### Indicator 2.3: Limitations in procurement bidding
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** Decree No. 73/2019/ND-CP on management of state investment in information technology application (Nghị định số 73/2019/NĐ-CP của Chính phủ: Quy định quản lý đầu tư ứng dụng công nghệ thông tin sử dụng nguồn vốn ngân sách nhà nước)
- **Evidence/Impact:** The Decree No. 73/2019/ND-CP emphasize the preference for domestically produced IT products and services. Article 4. Implementation of regulations on incentives for investment in and leasing and purchase of domestically manufactured information technology products and services.
 
The Ministry of Information and Communications shall promulgate the specific criteria for determination and announcement of the list of domestically manufactured IT products and services whose development, leasing and purchase are prioritized; list of shared open-source products qualified for use in state agencies; list of enterprises whose products meet the criteria for giving priority to investment, leasing and purchase. For specialized-use software, the Ministry of Information and Communications shall cooperate and reach an agreement with relevant Ministries before promulgation of criteria.”
This regulation was explained by the Ministry of Information and Communications to the Government during the drafting process as follows:
The prioritization requirement under this Decree applies exclusively to state agencies and public procurement, and is consistent with Vietnam’s international commitments:

• Under the CPTPP, domestic preference remains permissible for a certain period. Specifically, for central-level agencies, tenders valued below approximately USD 2 million are excluded from CPTPP obligations during the first five years of the Agreement’s implementation. The EU-Vietnam Free Trade Agreement (EVFTA) also allows for similar thresholds and transitional periods to favor domestic goods.

• Regarding the WTO Government Procurement Agreement (GPA), Vietnam is currently an observer and has not acceded to the Agreement. Therefore, prioritizing domestically produced goods in public procurement does not violate WTO rules.
Additionally, the proposal to include this provision, made by the Department of Information Technology (now the Authority of ICT Industry), was based on the following policy documents:
• Resolution No. 36-NQ/TW dated July 1, 2014, of the Politburo (11th tenure) on promoting IT application and development to support sustainable development and international integration;
• Directive No. 13/CT-TTg dated April 4, 2017, by the Prime Minister on promoting the use of domestically produced materials and goods in procurement for development and regular spending funded by the state budget;
• Directive No. 15/CT-TTg dated June 17, 2014, by the Prime Minister on strengthening cybersecurity and information safety.
The issuance of this regulation aims to realize key Party and Government policies by creating favorable market conditions for domestic IT enterprises to grow, gain market share, master core technologies, and expand exports. It also seeks to promote research, production, and the development of high-quality Vietnamese IT products and services.

In short, while Article 4 of Decree No. 73/2019/ND-CP emphasizes the prioritization of domestically produced IT products in state-funded procurement, this provision is limited to public sector projects and is designed to support domestic innovation and industry growth. It is implemented in a manner that aligns with Vietnam's international commitments, such as the CPTPP and EVFTA, which allow certain transitional preferences and thresholds for local content. Thus, while it may be perceived as creating competitive advantages for local suppliers, it does not constitute a violation of Vietnam’s trade obligations.

- **Evidence/Impact:** Article 42(4) of Law No. 07/2017/QH14 focuses on the evaluation and assessment of new technologies and products developed in Viet Nam, particularly those emerging from scientific research and technological advancements. The article emphasizes that these locally developed technologies and products should be appraised based on their price and quality, with the goal of ensuring they are comparable to imported alternatives. The broader aim is to encourage the use of these homegrown innovations in state-funded investment projects and the procurement of public goods and services, provided they meet the necessary standards of price and quality. This provision indirectly supports a preference for local content by promoting the adoption of domestically developed technologies when they are competitive with foreign counterparts.

Section 2.3.4 appropriately cites Clause 4, Article 42 of the law which addresses the evaluation and appraisal of new technologies and products developed in Viet Nam through scientific research and technological development. These technologies and products, when comparable in price and quality to imported counterparts, are encouraged for use in investment projects funded by the state budget and prioritized in public procurement bidding for the supply of products and public services. This provision indirectly supports the adoption of domestically developed technologies that are competitive with foreign equivalents. However, specific legal guidance on verifying the equivalence of price and quality with imported technologies and products has yet to be established.

Article 9 of the law encourages the transfer of advanced, clean, and nationally significant technologies, which can create a preference in public procurement for suppliers who use or develop these prioritized technologies.

### Indicator 3.1: Foreign equity limits in sectors relevant to digital trade 
Exception: Not cover foreign equity cap in telecom sector (covered under Pillar 5) and e-commerce sector (covered under Pillar 12)
- **Score:** 1
- **Source Legislation:** Law No. 61/2020/QH14 on Investment  (Luật số 61/2020/QH14 của Quốc hội: Luật Đầu tư) ;

Decree No. 31/2021/ND-CP (Nghị định số 31/2021/NĐ-CP của Chính phủ: Quy định chi tiết và hướng dẫn thi hành một số điều của Luật Đầu tư)
- **Evidence/Impact:** According to Clause 2, Article 9 of the 2020 Investment Law (Law No. 61/2020/QH14) and Clause 1, Article 15 of Decree No. 31/2021/ND-CP dated 26 March 2021—which provides detailed regulations and guidance for implementing certain provisions of the Investment Law—the conditions for market access for foreign investors are specified in Vietnam’s domestic legislation and in international treaties on investment to which Vietnam is a party. These include laws, resolutions of the National Assembly, ordinances, decrees, and treaties.

Article 23(1)(a) of the Law No. 61/2020/QH14 on Investment states that a business organization is considered a "foreign-invested business" if foreign investors hold more than 50% of its charter capital. This means that a company with over 50% foreign ownership is classified as a foreign investor, and must comply with the investment procedures and conditions applicable to foreign investors. This is a shift from previous regulations, where the threshold for foreign ownership was set at 51%. Now, any company with foreign ownership exceeding 50% is subject to the rules and restrictions that apply to foreign investors, including conditions for market access and investment registration.

Decree No. 31/2021/ND-CP further provides guidance on implementation and outlines the List of Restricted Sectors for Foreign Investors in Annex I, divided into:
1. A Prohibited List of 25 sectors completely closed to foreign investment, and
2. A Conditional Market Access List of 59 sectors, where foreign investors may enter but must meet specific conditions.

Decree No. 31/2021/ND-CP  provides guidance on the implementation of the Law No. 61/2020/QH14 on Investment. This regulation delineates sectors where foreign investors are subject to market access restrictions, divided into the Market Entry list (59 sectors where foreign investors must satisfy certain conditions to enter the market) and the Prohibition List (25 sectors completely closed to foreign investments)​. 

According to Section A of Appendix I of Decree 31/2021, foreign investors are not allowed to invest in these fields: 
-Trade in goods and services on the list of goods and services under state monopoly;
-Press activities and collection of news (Activities related to journalism,news gathering, and mass media in any shape or form are prohibited for foreign investment) ; 
- Public survey services;
- Merchanting trade of goods;
- Temporary import for re-export of goods;
- Export, import, distribute some goods on the list of goods which foreign investors and foreign-invested enterprises do not have the right to export, import or distribute;
- Industrial property representative services and intellectual property assessment services;
- Travel services, except international travel services for inbound tourists;

According to Section B of Appendix I of the Decree, foreign investors are authorized to invest in these fields, but need to meet a certain amount of conditions (the exact foeign equity cap is not mentioned):
-Postal and telecommunications services
- Advertising services, including digital advertising
- Publishing services, relevant to digital content distribution, have conditional market access.
- E-commerce activities

Notably, “electronic commerce activities” are the only sector explicitly referenced in the context of digital commerce. The list does not mention broader “digital commerce” sectors. While Vietnam’s WTO services commitments do not impose any specific limitation on foreign ownership in e-commerce, actual market access conditions are governed by domestic legislation, including Decree No. 85/2021/ND-CP, and fall under the regulatory authority of the Ministry of Industry and Trade.

Article 5.3 of the IP Law states: “Intellectual property rights of foreign organizations and individuals shall be protected in Vietnam on the basis of treaties to which the Socialist Republic of Vietnam is a contracting party, or on the principle of reciprocity.” As a member of the WTO, Vietnam is bound by the TRIPS Agreement, under which Article 3 (National Treatment) prohibits member states from discriminating between nationals and foreigners in the protection and enforcement of intellectual property rights.

Under Article 86(a) of Law No. 07/2022/QH15, the patent owner holds the exclusive right to prevent others from making, using, selling, or importing the patented product or process without consent. This provision is designed to safeguard the patent owner’s interests and provides a solid legal basis for action against infringement. Article 125 of Law No. 07/2022/QH15 further clarifies that any unauthorized use of a patented invention constitutes an infringement. This enables the patent owner to initiate lawsuits in Vietnamese courts. The law thus supports the enforcement of patent rights through judicial remedies, including injunctions to halt infringing acts and claims for compensation.

Additionally, Article 204 of the same law provides for a wide range of compensation options, including financial damages and other penalties the court may impose for willful infringement. These provisions serve to ensure that patent rights are enforceable in practice and that infringers are held accountable. Article 1.3 of the amended IP Law (2022) reinforces the principle of non-discrimination by affirming that IP rights—including patents—are protected equally for both Vietnamese and foreign entities, provided legal requirements are met. The enforcement procedures under Vietnamese law are transparent, fair, and accessible. The legal framework allows for both judicial and administrative avenues to be pursued without discrimination or undue restrictions.

Article 200 of the Law on Intellectual Property No. 50/2005/QH11 provides that organizations and individuals holding IP rights are entitled to request competent authorities to handle acts of infringement. This right is not subject to any conditions or limitations regarding the nationality or origin of the right holder.

Moreover, Article 202 outlines a range of civil remedies available to right holders, such as compensation, termination of infringing acts, and destruction of infringing goods. These remedies are enforced through open court proceedings governed by the Civil Procedure Code, which mandates public hearings—except in cases involving state secrets or sensitive personal matters—ensuring transparency.

Administrative enforcement is also available under Decree No. 99/2013/ND-CP (as amended by Decree No. 126/2021/ND-CP and Decree No. 46/2024/ND-CP). This mechanism allows right holders to initiate actions through agencies such as market surveillance, customs, and police. These procedures are well-regulated, and decisions made by administrative authorities are subject to appeal and review, which upholds legal certainty and procedural clarity.

Therefore, the assertion that “patent enforcement is not transparent” is not supported by the current legal framework in Vietnam. On the contrary, Vietnam’s IP laws are aligned with its international obligations under the TRIPS Agreement and are designed to provide effective, fair, and non-discriminatory enforcement of intellectual property rights.

There are no formal legal restrictions under Vietnamese law that limit a patent holder’s ability to enforce their rights. The framework clearly provides for enforcement through civil, administrative, and provisional measures. These are detailed in the IP Law No. 50/2005/QH11 (as amended), particularly in Articles 198, 202, and 208–210. Right holders are thus empowered to seek remedies such as termination of infringement, compensation, destruction of infringing goods, and the application of provisional measures.

For instance, Article 198.1 of the IP Law affirms that IP holders have the right to “apply technological measures, request competent authorities to handle acts of infringement, and initiate lawsuits at court to protect their lawful rights and interests.” Likewise, Article 202 guarantees a range of civil remedies without imposing any restrictions on access to enforcement mechanisms.

In summary, Vietnam’s legal framework does not impose restrictions on patent enforcement. On the contrary, it offers a comprehensive set of remedies and sanctions to safeguard patent rights and deter infringement, as outlined above.

Article 127 further enumerates specific acts of infringement, including unauthorized acquisition, use, and disclosure of trade secrets.
For a trade secret to be eligible for protection, Article 84(A) states that it must not be common knowledge or easily obtainable, must provide a competitive edge in business, and the owner must take necessary measures to maintain its secrecy to prevent unauthorized access or disclosure.

In terms of enforcement, Article 127(1) of Law No. 50/2005/QH11outlines what constitutes an infringement of trade secret rights. Infringement includes unauthorized access, acquisition, or use of a trade secret by bypassing security measures, as well as the disclosure of a trade secret without the owner’s consent, constitute violations. Furthermore, this article includes actions like breaching security contracts, deception, bribery, or other unethical means used to access or disclose a trade secret as forms of infringement.

Article 125(3) of Law No. 50/2005/QH11outlines exceptions to these protections, stating that the rights to a trade secret do not apply in cases where the information is used without knowledge of its illegal acquisition, disclosed to protect public interest, used for non-commercial purposes, independently discovered, or reverse-engineered. These provisions ensure a balanced approach, protecting the interests of trade secret holders while allowing for legitimate uses under specific circumstances.

The 2019 Amending Law strengthens enforcement by aligning Vietnamese IP regulations with international commitments, especially under the CPTPP and EVFTA, emphasizing clearer definitions and enforcement procedures regarding undisclosed information.

In addition, Resolution No. 08/2021/NQ-UBTVQH15—which interprets Clause 1 of Article 289 of the Criminal Code—clarifies criminal liability for unauthorized access to computer networks, telecommunications networks, or electronic devices. This provides a criminal enforcement basis for trade secret violations conducted via digital platforms, consistent with international obligations. Furthermore, Decree No. 99/2013/ND-CP, as amended by Decree No. 126/2021/ND-CP and Decree No. 46/2024/ND-CP, establishes a comprehensive administrative sanctioning framework in the field of industrial property. Clause 15a of Article 14 sets out specific administrative penalties for trade secret infringement, including monetary fines, confiscation of infringing materials, and temporary suspension of business activities.

The "Safe Harbor" provision in the revised Intellectual Property Law of Viet Nam, particularly Article 198b, establishes a legal framework that limits the liability of internet intermediaries such as ISPs (Intermediary Service Providers) and OSPs (Online Service Providers) for copyright infringements committed by their users.

Article 198b (Legal liability of intermediary service providers regarding copyright and related rights) applies specifically to digitalized works, performances, phonograms, video recordings (audio and visual fixation), and broadcasts that are copyrightable. This means that ISPs and OSPs cannot be held liable for the infringement of other forms of Intellectual Property (IP) protection such as trademarks, counterfeit marks, geographical indications, industrial designs, or patents. Under Article 198b, the law recognizes three main types of intermediary services eligible for liability exemptions: the transmission of user information (referred to as "mere conduit" services), automatic and temporary storage of data to make transmission more efficient (known as "caching"), and the storage of user-generated content at the request of users (referred to as "hosting"). The article specifies that ISPs and OSPs can only benefit from these exemptions if they act as neutral conduits and do not play an active role in the creation or selection of the infringing content.

To qualify for safe harbor protection, intermediaries must take certain steps, such as promptly responding to notifications of copyright infringement. This involves removing or disabling access to infringing content upon learning about its unlawful nature. The law mandates that intermediaries implement technical measures to protect copyright and related rights and cooperate with state agencies and relevant stakeholders in enforcing these measures. The provision ensures that intermediaries are only protected when they do not have knowledge of the infringing activity or, upon gaining such knowledge, act expeditiously to address it.

### Indicator 12.8: Lack of legal framework for online consumer protection
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** Law No. 20/2023/QH15, on E-Transactions (Luật số 20/2023/QH15 của Quốc hội: Luật Giao dịch điện tử)
- **Evidence/Impact:** The Law on E-Transactions (Law 20/2023/QH15) also sets a legal framework to support consumer protection within e-transactions, though it focuses on establishing validity, integrity, and security measures over specific consumer protection rights.

The law ensures that data messages, a central component of e-commerce, are legally valid and can serve as documents, originals, or even evidence in legal proceedings (Articles 7–11). This guarantees that consumers’ transactions conducted via data messages (such as contracts or receipts) hold the same enforceability as traditional documents, giving consumers assurance that online agreements are binding and reliable. Article 8 explicitly states that data messages cannot be invalidated solely based on their electronic form, while Article 10 confirms their use as originals, provided the data remains unaltered.

The law includes provisions on e-signatures, enhancing consumer protection by establishing conditions for authenticity and non-repudiation (Article 23). By recognizing digital signatures as equivalent to handwritten signatures, consumers are assured that their consent in transactions is verifiable and legally binding. 

The law supports the validity of e-contracts, enabling consumers to engage in online contracts with the same legal protections as traditional contracts (Articles 34–38). The law outlines steps for the entry, execution, and acknowledgment of e-contracts, specifying that data messages exchanged in this context have full legal validity as traditional notifications or records.
