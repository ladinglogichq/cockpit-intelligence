### Indicator 2.1: Foreign exclusions from public procurement related to ICT goods and digital services
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** Public Procurement Rules 2004;

Mechanism for Blacklisting and Debarment of Bidders or Contractors 2024
- **Evidence/Impact:** Regulation 5 states that if Pakistan's public procurement rules conflict with international agreements or treaties that the Federal Government is part of, those agreements will take precedence. 

Under Rule 14(a), procurements related to national security can be excluded from public advertisement. This gives the government discretion to exclude foreign firms from ICT and digital service procurements that are deemed critical to national security.

Accroding to section 4 of the Mechanism for Blacklisting and Debarment of Bidders or Contractors 2024, the procuring agency shall constitute and notify a Committee comprising of an odd number of persons, not less than three members, as soon as the procurement process is initiated, to conduct blacklisting or barring action against the bidder or contractor.
Section 5, the reasons for Blacklisting and Debarment:
(a) Blacklisting & debarment on account of involvement in corrupt & fraudulent practices. If a procuring agency has sufficient reasons to believe that a bidder or contractor is involved in the corrupt and fraudulent practices as mentioned in Rule-2(f)(g) to (v) of the Public Procurement Rules, 2004, the Procuring agency shall blacklist and henceforth cross debar the bidder or contractor for participation in any public procurement or disposal proceedings for the period of not more than ten (10) years.
(b) Blacklisting & debarment on account of failure to perform contractual obligations:
(i) The blacklisting committee shall initiate blacklisting proceedings for a period of not more than three (3) years if the contractor failed to perform his contractual obligations during the execution of contract or breaches the contract due to his incapacity and incapability to perform or otherwise.
(ii) Procuring agency shall initiate such blacklisting or debarment proceedings after exhausting the forum of arbitration, provided that such provision exists in the conditions of contract, and if such failure or breach is covered in the respective dispute settlement clauses of the contract.
(c) Blacklisting & debarment on account of failure to abide by bid securing declaration. The Blacklisting Committee shall initiate blacklisting and henceforth cross debarment for participation in respective category of public procurement or disposal proceedings for a period of not more than six months if the bidder fails to abide with a bid securing declaration without being indulged in any corrupt and fraudulent practices.
In practice, various procuring agencies reported the domestic and foreign firms which they have blacklisted/debarred permanently or temporarily to PPRA.

### Indicator 4.2: Patent enforcement issues: civil and administrative procedures and remedies, provisional and other measures
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** Patent Rules 2003
- **Evidence/Impact:** Rule 18(Opposition to Grant of Patent) states that a notice of opposition to the grant of a patent must be in the prescribed form (Form P-7). The opposition must state the grounds on which the opponent intends to oppose the grant. It must be accompanied by a statement setting out the opponent’s interest, the facts relied upon, and the relief sought. The applicant has the opportunity to file a counterstatement within two months of receiving the opposition notice.
Rule 26(Revocation of Patents)  states that an application for revocation must be made in the prescribed form (Form P-14), stating the grounds for the application. A copy of the application and a statement setting out the applicant’s interests and the facts relied upon must be sent to the patentee.
Rule 27(Surrender of Patent) states that a patentee shall offer to surrender a patent by giving notice in the prescribed form (Form P-15). Any person may give notice of opposition to the surrender within two months from the advertisement of the offer.
Rule 28(Amendment of Specification) states that an application to amend a complete specification must be made in the prescribed form (Form P-16) and must be advertised in the Official Gazette.
Any person wishing to oppose the amendment has two months from the date of the advertisement to give notice to the Controller.
Rule 32(Non-Voluntary License) states that an application for a non-voluntary license must be made in the prescribed form (Form P-22) and must be accompanied by a statement of facts and evidence. The Controller may, after hearing the applicant, decide whether to grant the non-voluntary license.This could be considered a provisional measure to promote public interest.
Rule 31 (Correction of Clerical Errors) states that requests for the correction of clerical errors must be made in the prescribed form (Form P-21). The Controller may advertise the request and the nature of the proposed correction in the Official Gazette. Any person may give notice of opposition to the proposed correction within two months from the date of the advertisement.
Rule 25(Restoration of Lapsed Patents) states that an application for the restoration of a lapsed patent must be made in the prescribed form (Form P-13) and must be accompanied by evidence in support of the statements made in the application. If opposition is given, the provisions of sub-rules (3) to (12) of rule 18 shall apply.
- **Timeframe:** Since December 2003
- **References:** https://wipolex-res.wipo.int/edocs/lexdocs/laws/en/pk/pk015en.pdf

### Indicator 4.2: Patent enforcement issues: civil and administrative procedures and remedies, provisional and other measures
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** Patent Ordinance 2000
- **Evidence/Impact:** Regarding Civil and Administrative Procedures and Remedies:
Section 23 allows any person to oppose the grant of a patent within four months from the date of advertisement of the acceptance of a complete specification. The opposition can be based on various grounds, including non-disclosure, lack of clarity in the specification, or extending beyond the scope of the disclosure.
Section 46 states that a patent may be revoked by the High Court on the petition of any person interested or of the Federal Government, on various grounds such as the patent being obtained through concealment or misrepresentation, or if the patentee has failed to comply with the request of the Federal Government to make, use, or exercise the patented invention.
Section 47 allows that within twelve months after the sealing of a patent, any person interested can apply to the Controller for an order revoking the patent on grounds such as the invention not being new, lacking an inventive step, or not being capable of industrial application.
According to Section 48, the Federal Government may declare a patent revoked if it is mischievous to the State, prejudicial to the public, obtained through concealment or misrepresentation, or if the compulsory license granted to prevent abuse has not been sufficient.
Section 49, a patentee may offer to surrender a patent by giving notice to the Controller, which can be opposed by any interested person within a prescribed period.
Section 58, the Federal Government may authorize the exploitation of a patented invention by a government agency or a third person, even without the consent of the patent owner, under certain circumstances such as public interest, non-exploitation by the patentee, or anti-competitive practices.
Section 59, the Controller may issue a non-voluntary license to prevent abuses that might result from the exercise of patent rights, such as failure to work the patent or insufficient exploitation.
Section 60, a patentee may institute a suit in a District Court against any person who makes, sells, or uses the invention without a license, or counterfeits it.
Section 61 outlines the remedies available in patent infringement lawsuits, including the power of the court to grant relief by way of damages, injunctions, or accounts.
Section 62 provides circumstances under which damages shall not be awarded against a defendant in patent infringement proceedings, if the defendant can prove lack of knowledge or reasonable grounds to believe that the patent existed.
Section 63 deals with relief for infringement of partially valid specifications, allowing the court to grant relief in respect of any valid claim that is infringed.
Section 64 gives exclusive licensees the right to take proceedings in respect of any infringement of the patent committed after the date of the license.

Regarding Provisional Measures:
Section 61 also mentions that the court may order effective provisional measures to prevent an infringement and to preserve relevant evidence regarding the alleged infringement.
Section 62(2) involves the court's authority to order provisional measures, where appropriate, to prevent the entry of infringing goods into the channels of commerce and to preserve evidence, particularly where there is a risk of irreparable harm or evidence being destroyed.
