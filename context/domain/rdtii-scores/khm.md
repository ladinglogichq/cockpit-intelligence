### Indicator 4.2: Patent enforcement issues: civil and administrative procedures and remedies, provisional and other measures
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** Law on the Patents, Utility Model Certificates and Industrial Designs 2003; 

Code of Civil Procedure of Cambodia 2006
- **Evidence/Impact:** The owner of the patent shall have the right (...) to institute court proceedings against any person who infringes the patent by performing, without his agreement, any of the acts referred in Article 42 of this Law or who performs acts which make it
likely that infringement will occur (Article 43). The owner of an industrial design shall have the right to institute Competent Court proceedings against any person who infringes his industrial design by performing, without the agreement, any of the acts referred to in Article 106 of this Law or who performs acts which make it likely that infringement will occur (Article 108).

IPR holders that suffer damage from an infringement are entitled to file civil claims for damages and specific relief from the infringer. The Cambodian Civil Code and the Civil Procedure Code provide procedures for civil remedies and provisional measures against IPR infringements in Cambodia. Civil proceedings begin with a statement of claims (lawsuit), which is to be filed with the competent court in Cambodia. Usually, the residence/location of the defendant will establish the jurisdiction.

The Cambodian Laws empowers its court to issue injunctions to halt infringement or impending infringement of patents, but leaves the criteria for obtaining injunctions undefined. However, under Cambodia’s Civil Procedure Code, the court can grant preliminary/temporary relief, pending a final judgment, when the plaintiff establishes a strong prima facie case on the merits, as well as a compelling argument that if the defendant is permitted to continue infringing the plaintiff’s design during the pendency of the action, it will be unable to recover damages. 

The Civil Code provides IP rights holders with civil and administrative procedures to address infringements, including remedies for enforcing specific obligations and seeking damages. According to Article 396 of the Cambodian Civil Code, an IP rights holder can request that the court enforce specific performance if the infringer fails to fulfill legal obligations, unless the nature of the obligation prevents this. Additionally, Articles 398 and 399 allow IP owners to claim damages if they suffer harm due to an infringement, provided the infringement was not caused by unavoidable circumstances. Here, compensation is calculated based on the damages sustained from the infringement.

The Civil Code also permits provisional measures to secure IP rights before a final judgment is issued. Article 545 allows for provisional attachment when there’s a risk that enforcing a claim may become difficult. This measure restricts the infringer’s access to certain assets, preventing their transfer or concealment. Further, Article 548 grants courts the authority to issue a provisional disposition over disputed assets, which may limit any alterations to the IP or infringing goods. As specified in Article 549, provisional disposition can involve restraining the infringer from taking specific actions or appointing a custodian to oversee the property at issue. If the infringer disputes these provisional measures, Article 550 allows them to file a motion of objection, which the court may use to reassess or modify the relief granted.

For enforcement, the Civil Code outlines procedures to execute provisional attachments against infringers’ assets to secure the IP holder’s claims effectively. Articles 567 and 568 detail the execution process against various types of assets, such as immovable property or vessels, thereby allowing IP holders to secure financial claims against substantial assets of the infringer.

By making it illegal to create, distribute, or use circumvention tools, Article 62 supports DRM (Digital Rights Management) systems used widely on digital content platforms to prevent unauthorized copying, limit access to subscribed or purchased content, and control how content can be used or shared. 

Article 62 also prohibits the removal or alteration of rights management information (RMI), which includes details about the author, copyright holder, licensing terms, or usage conditions that are typically embedded in digital content files. RMI allows authors and copyright holders to maintain traceability and enforceability of their rights across digital platforms, as it informs users about the legal restrictions and ownership details of a digital work.
Unauthorized modification or removal of this information can lead to copyright infringement by concealing ownership or licensing terms. Protecting RMI ensures that copyright holders can better monitor and claim their rights, even when content is shared or distributed online.

Articles 57 and 58 empower rights holders to seek judicial remedies, including injunctions, damages, and orders for the confiscation or destruction of infringing materials. These articles allow copyright holders to pursue compensation for unauthorized use and have infringing copies removed from circulation, whether online or offline.

Customs enforcement (Article 63) also allows rights holders to request border control measures to prevent infringing goods, including pirated digital media on physical storage devices, from entering or leaving Cambodia. This can help limit the physical import or export of unauthorized reproductions and protect copyright on a broader scale.

Article 59 further allows for provisional measures to preserve evidence, which is particularly useful in the digital realm where infringement can be transient. Courts may order the seizure of digital storage devices or temporary restrictions on online access to prevent the destruction of evidence.
