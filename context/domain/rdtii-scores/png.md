Moreover, regarding the exploitation, Papua New Guinea permits the use of patent interventions under Section 32 (Exploitation by Government or person authorized by Government), Division 5 (Exploitation by Government or person authorized by Government) under Part 3. However, such interventions are in align with WTO TRIPS exception. Specifically, the Minister in Papua New Guinea has the authority to permit the exploitation of a patented invention without the patent owner's consent under certain conditions. This can occur when it's necessary for public interest, such as national security, health, or economic development, or when the current exploitation by the patent owner or licensee is deemed anti-competitive. Before granting such authorization, the Minister must ensure that the patent owner has been approached for a contractual license and that such a license could not be obtained on reasonable terms. The patent owner and any interested parties must also be given an opportunity to be heard, except in cases of national or extreme emergencies, where the owner will be notified as soon as practicable. The authorized exploitation will be limited to the specified purpose and requires the payment of adequate remuneration to the patent owner. Additionally, the Minister’s decision must account for the economic value of the use and, if applicable, the need to address anti-competitive practices. 

Note for more information, please see Section 27 (Exploitation of patented invention by Government or by third persons authorized by Government) stated in the Patents and Industrial Designs Regulation 2002.

### Indicator 4.5: Online copyright enforcement issues: civil and administrative procedures and remedies; and provisional measures
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** Copyright and Neighbouring Rights Act No.21 of 2000
- **Evidence/Impact:** Part IV of the Copyright and Neighbouring Rights Act prescribes enforcement measures for protecting copyright and neighbouring rights. The law addresses conservatory and provisional measures, civil remedies, criminal sanctions, and measures, remedies and sanctions against abuses (Sections 26-29). 

Section 26 (Conservatory and provisional measures) grants the Court authority to issue injunctions, impound unauthorized copies, and seize related implements and documents. The provisions of the Criminal Code Act and Customs Act are applicable to these infringements. Section 27 (Civil remedies) allows right holders to claim damages, seek destruction of infringing copies, and request cessation of ongoing infringements. If an infringer is unaware of their infringement, damages may be limited to the infringer's profits. Section 28 (Criminal sanctions) introduces criminal sanctions for willful or grossly negligent infringement, with penalties including imprisonment or fines. 

Notably, Section 29 (Measures, remedies and sanctions against abuses) is applicable to address online infringement. Actions are deemed unlawful and equated to infringements. These include the manufacture or importation of devices designed to bypass copyright protection, assist in receiving encrypted programs without authorization, or remove/alter electronic rights management information. Additionally, distributing or broadcasting works with removed or altered rights management information without permission is also prohibited. These activities are treated as copyright or neighboring rights infringements, subject to the civil remedies and criminal sanctions detailed in Sections 26, 27, and 28.
- **Timeframe:** Since 2000
- **References:** http://www.paclii.org/pg/legis/consol_act/canra2000341/index.html#p3
