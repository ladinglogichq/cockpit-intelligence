### Indicator 1.4: Imposing trade defence measures (anti-dumping, countervailing duties and safeguards) on ICT-related products imported by other Asia-Pacific economies
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** Anti-dumping Law
- **Evidence/Impact:** Anti-dumping measures aim to protect Myanmar's domestic producers from the adverse effects of dumping by foreign exporters. Under Myanmar's Anti-Dumping Law (ADL), dumping is defined as an increase in the volume of imported goods relative to domestic production, differing from the WTO's definition, which focuses on price discrimination. The ADL establishes an anti-dumping committee responsible for conducting investigations upon receiving applications from domestic producers. According to the Anti-dumping Law, a domestic producer who suffers material injury due to dumped imported goods may apply for anti-dumping measures to be imposed by an anti-dumping committee.

### Indicator 4.4: Lack of copyright framework and exceptions
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** Copyright Law
(Pyidaungsu Hluttaw Law No.15/2019)
- **Evidence/Impact:** The Copyright Law enacted since March 2019 and came into effect on 31 October 2023. Myanmar adopts clear copyright exceptions following fair use regime (Chapter XII Limitations and exceptions of economic rights). According to Section 24, it allows individuals to reproduce parts of published works for personal use without authorization, provided it does not conflict with the normal exploitation of the work or damage the legitimate interests of the right holder. This exemption does not apply to the reproduction of work in: i) architecture such as building, ii) musical work, iii) whole or part of a database in the digital platform, and iv) computer program. 

Section 26 stipulates that reproduction in the form of quotations from a published work is allowed if it complies with fair practice, does not exceed what is necessary for the purpose, and is properly attributed. Section 27 includes the use of parts of a work for educational purposes, particularly in course materials, whether in physical or electronic form. Libraries and archives may reproduce works for preservation or research, provided certain conditions are met, such as non-commercial use (Section 28).
- **Timeframe:** Since October 2023

### Indicator 5.1: Absence of passive infrastructure sharing
- **Score:** 0.5
- **Source Legislation:** Telecommunications Law (Pyidaungsu Hluttaw Law No. 31/2013), as amended by the Pyi Htaung Su Hluttaw Law No. 26/2017;

Universal Service Strategy for Myanmar (2024-2026) published by the Posts and Telecommunications Department, Ministry of Transport and Communications 2024
- **Evidence/Impact:** Under the Telecommunications Law, there is no obligation for passive infrastructure sharing in Myanmar to deliver telecom services to end users. Instead, it permits infrastructure sharing on a voluntary basis, subject to regulatory approval. For instance, Section 11(b) stipulates that the licence holder may enter into agreements with any other licence holder for the following purposes of sharing of network facilities. Section 33, a service licence holder may, where requested by another service licence holder, enter into an agreement with the requesting licence holder for the access to and interconnection of network facilities and network services. The agreement shall be made with the approval of the Department.

However, it is practiced in the mobile sector and in the fixed sector based on commercial agreements. According to the report published by the Posts and Telecommunications Department, Myanmar’s market is characterised by the strong presence of several independent tower companies. Tower companies have contracts for building and operating sites for the Nationwide Telecommunications Licence holders. These tower sites are typically shared by two or more service providers. This passive infrastructure sharing reduces costs significantly.
