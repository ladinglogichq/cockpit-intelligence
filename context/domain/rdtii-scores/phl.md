### Indicator 1.4: Imposing trade defence measures (anti-dumping, countervailing duties and safeguards) on ICT-related products imported by other Asia-Pacific economies
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** The Republic Act 8752 - The Anti-Dumping Act of 1999;

The Republic Act 8800 - Safeguard Measures Act of 2000;

The Republic Act 8751 - Countervailing Duty Act of 1999;

The Republic Act No. 10863 -
Customs Modernization and Tariff Act of 2016
- **Evidence/Impact:** The Customs Modernization and Tariff Act (Republic Act No. 10863) incorporated trade remedy provisions but primarily focused on modernizing customs and tariff administration, streamlining trade procedures, and improving compliance.

Trade remedies for anti-dumping, countervailing duties, and safeguards are available under Philippine laws—Republic Acts 8752, 8751, and 8800, respectively:
- The Anti-Dumping Act (RA 8752) allows for the imposition of duties on products sold below their normal value, addressing situations where dumping causes material injury to local producers (Section 3).
- The Countervailing Duty Act (RA 8751) enables the government to impose duties on subsidized imports that harm local industries, ensuring fair competition by neutralizing the benefits of foreign subsidies (Section 4).
- The Safeguard Measures Act (RA 8800) offers immediate relief to industries threatened by a sudden surge in imports, regardless of whether the imports are fairly traded (Section 5), allowing the government to act swiftly to protect jobs and industries.

Since the implementation of the Philippine trade remedy laws in 1999 and 2000, the Department of Trade and Industry (DTI) has not ordered any anti-dumping, countervailing, or safeguard measures on digital goods. The Philippines maintains a non-restrictive policy environment for tariffs and trade defense measures applied on intraregional imports of ICT-related goods.

As of May 2025, based on information from the WTO and the DTI Philippines portal, the Philippines has implemented both anti-dumping and safeguard measures on other kinds of products:
- Anti-dumping duties have been applied to products such as cement from Vietnam, gypsum board from Thailand, and wheat flour from Türkiye.
- For safeguard measures, the Philippines has:
- Imposed a definitive safeguard duty on high-density polyethylene (HDPE),
- Imposed a provisional safeguard duty on cement, and
- Initiated an investigation on corrugating medium imports from various countries.
- The safeguard investigation on LPG cylinders was terminated in 2024, and no definitive safeguard duty was imposed.

While foreign bids/bidders are allowed, this is subject to undergoing registration with the Philippine Government Electronic Procurement System (PhilGEPS) and submission of eligibility requirements/documents/statements that are applicable for all bids/bidders regardless of nationality (as specified under Rule III, Section 20.2.9.2 of the IRR).

c) When the goods sought to be procured are not available from local suppliers; or
d) When there is a need to prevent situations that defeat competition or restrain trade.
For iinvestment projects, they are open when provided for under any treaty or international or executive agreement as provided in Section 4 of the Act and this IRR.

Moreover, for consulting services, foreign consultants may only be hired in the event Filipino consultants do not have the sufficient expertise and capability to render the services required (as specified under Rule VII, Section 52.4.3.3).

### Indicator 2.3: Limitations in procurement bidding
- **Score:** 1
- **Source Legislation:** The Republic Act 12009 - New Government Procurement Act (NGPA);

The Implementing Rules and Regulations of Republic Act 12009 or the New Government Procurement Act (IRR)
- **Evidence/Impact:** Under Rule XIII of the IRR of RA No. 12009, procurement of goods may be sourced from either domestic or foreign suppliers, with participation open to all eligible suppliers, manufacturers, and distributors (Section 78). However, Section 79 establishes a framework for domestic preference. Specifically, the Procuring Entity is required to give priority and preference to Philippine products and services throughout the procurement process, including raw materials, ingredients, supplies, and fixtures (Section 79.1). For a period of ten (10) years from the effectivity of RA No. 11981 or the Tatak Pinoy (Proudly Filipino) Act, and for sectors covered by the prevailing Tatak Pinoy Strategy, the Procuring Entity must award the contract to a domestic bidder (Section 79.2). A domestic bidder is defined as any person or entity offering goods that are either unmanufactured products of Philippine origin or manufactured products substantially made from Philippine-grown, produced, or manufactured components.

After this ten-year period, domestic preference will continue through a margin of preference to be determined by the Tatak Pinoy Council, which shall not be lower than fifteen percent (15%) (Section 79.3). For goods and services under the Tatak Pinoy Strategy (TPS), the applicable domestic preference rules will be based on the IRR or guidelines issued under the Tatak Pinoy Act (Section 79.4). Additionally, the Procuring Entity may still award the contract to a domestic bidder if their bid is not more than twenty-five percent (25%) higher than the lowest foreign bid, subject to periodic review by the Government Procurement Policy Board (GPPB) (Section 79.5). To claim such preference, domestic bidders must obtain a certification from the appropriate agency confirming that the goods in their bid are substantially composed of Philippine-made components (Section 79.6).

The domestic preference requirements may be waived under specific conditions, such as if domestic production is insufficient or unavailable in commercial quantities, if the required quality cannot be met, if applying domestic preference would conflict with international obligations, or under other analogous circumstances (Section 79.7).

### Indicator 3.1: Foreign equity limits in sectors relevant to digital trade 
Exception: Not cover foreign equity cap in telecom sector (covered under Pillar 5) and e-commerce sector (covered under Pillar 12)
- **Score:** 1
- **Source Legislation:** The Constitution of the Republic of the Philippines 1987;

The Republic Act No.7042 - Foreign Investment Act of 1991;

The Executive Order No. 175 - 12th Foreign Investment Negative List
- **Evidence/Impact:** The 1987 Philippine Constitution establishes foundational restrictions on foreign ownership across several critical sectors, reflecting the nation's policy of protecting industries considered vital to its sovereignty. Under Article XII, Section 2, foreign equity in the exploitation of natural resources is limited to 40%, ensuring Filipino citizens or corporations with at least 60% Filipino ownership maintain control. Article XII, Section 10 further restricts foreign ownership in enterprises related to national defense, security, and public utilities, where only 40% foreign equity is allowed. This is reinforced by Article XIV, Section 4(2), which caps foreign ownership of educational institutions at 40%, and Article XVI, Section 11, which prohibits foreign ownership of mass media, ensuring only Filipinos can own or manage these entities. 

The Foreign Investments Act of 1991 (Republic Act No. 7042, last amended by the Republc Act No.11647) provides a legal framework for foreign investments in the Philippines while also codifying the restrictions mandated by the Constitution. This act allows 100% foreign ownership in enterprises not restricted by law or the Constitution. However, Section 8 of the law requires the creation and periodic update of the Foreign Investment Negative List (FINL), which specifies the sectors where foreign ownership is either limited or prohibited. 

List A of the FINL enumerates industries where foreign ownership is limited due to constitutional or legislative mandates. For instance,
- No foreign equity: mass media, except recording and Internet business (Section 11, Article XVI of the 1987 Constitution).
- No foreign equity: practice of professions (Section 14, Article XII of the Constitution) except in cases specifically allowed by law following the prescribed conditions. Electrical engineering, electronics engineering, and electronics technician are not allowed foreigners to practice except if subject to reciprocity as provided in the pertinent laws.
- Up to 30% foreign equity: Advertising (Section 11, Article XVI of the 1987 Constitution).
- Up to 40% foreign equity: Private radio communication network (Section 11 of the 1987 Constitution, Natoinal Telecommunications Commission Memorandum Circular No.10-8-91).
Moreover, private security agencies and small-scale mining are under List A. No foreign equity is allowed in Private Security Agencies (Section 4 of RA 5487; Rule IV, Section 1 of the implementing rules and regulations of RA 5487), and no foreign equity is allowed in Small-scale mining (Section 3, RA No. 7076).

List B restricts foreign ownership in industries related to national security and public safety, including private security agencies and small-scale mining operations up to 40 %, in line with the Philippine Constitution. In this sense: 
- Micro and small domestic market enterprises with paid in equity capital of less than the equivalent of US$200,000 (RA No.7042 as amended by RA No.9487)
- Micro and small domestic market enterprises: (i) that involve advance technology as determined by the Department of Science and Technology (DOST); or (ii) are endorsed as startup or startup enablers by the lead host agecies, namely the Department of Trade and Industry, Department of Information and Communications Technology or DOST pursuant to RA No.11337 otherwise known as the Innovative Startup Act; or (iii) with a majority of direct employees as Filipinos but in no case shall the number of Filipino employees be less than 15 with paid-in equity capital of less than the equivalent of US$100,000 (RA No.7042 as amended by RA No.11647).

### Indicator 4.2: Patent enforcement issues: civil and administrative procedures and remedies, provisional and other measures
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** The Republic Act No. 8293 - Intellectual Property Code of the Philippines of 1997;

The Republic Act No. 10372 - An Act Amending Certain Provisions Of Republic Act No. 8293, Otherwise Known As The "Intellectual Property Code Of The Philippines," And For Other Purposes of 2013;
- **Evidence/Impact:** The legal process for addressing patent infringement begins with the filing of a complaint. According to Section 76 of the Intellectual Property Code, any person who uses, makes, sells, or imports a patented invention without the authorization of the patent holder during the term of the patent commits patent infringement. 

Remedies for patent infringement include civil and criminal options. Under Section 76.2, the patent holder can file a civil action to recover damages, attorney’s fees, and secure an injunction against further infringement. If actual damages are difficult to ascertain, the court may award reasonable royalties as per Section 76.3. In certain cases, damages may be tripled under Section 76.4, depending on the severity of the infringement. The court also has the authority to order the destruction or removal of infringing goods from the market (Section 76.5). Moreover, individuals who contribute to the infringement, such as by supplying components specifically intended for infringement, are also liable as contributory infringers under Section 76.6. For repeat offenders, Section 84 provides for criminal sanctions, including imprisonment of six months to three years, and fines between PHP 100,000 and PHP 300,000. 

Section 216 outlines the remedies for infringement and includes measures that can be deemed provisional such as injunctions [ 216.(a) ] that allow the court to issue injunctions in order to restrain the infringer from continuing their infringing activities. This means that even before the final resolution of the case, the court can order the infringer to cease their actions that violate copyright.

Foreign nationals or entities are also allowed to file infringement suits in the Philippines, even if they are not licensed to do business in the country, provided they hold valid patents, as per Section 77. In the case of process patents, Section 78 establishes that an identical product is presumed to have been produced using the patented process unless proven otherwise, shifting the burden of proof to the alleged infringer.

### Indicator 8.1: Lack of safe harbour for copyright infringements
- **Score:** 0.5
- **Source Legislation:** The Republic Act No. 8792 - Electronic Commerce Act of 2000;

The Republic Act No. 10175 - Cybercrime Prevention Act of 2012;

Intellectual Property Office of the Philippines (IPOPHL) No.2023-025 - Supplemental Rules to Memorandum Circular No. 2020-049 known as
the "Revised Rules of Procedure on Administrative Enforcement of Intellectual Property Rights (IPR), otherwise known as the Rules on Voluntary Administrative Site Blocking" of 2024
- **Evidence/Impact:** A basic legal framework on intermediary liability is absent in Philippine law and jurisprudence. Arguably, safe harbor clauses are present in the Electronic Commerce Act of 2000 (RA 8792) and the Cybercrime Prevention Act of 2012 (RA 10175), but these laws’ definition of an intermediary limits its capacity to provide a safe harbor. Particularly, RA 8792’s scope is limited to electronic documents, while RA 10175 is limited to cybercrimes defined under the law. This makes safe harbors in Philippine law sectoral or content-specific.

Under the Cybercrime Prevention Act of 2012 (Republic Act No. 10175), Section 9 establishes that intermediaries must comply with takedown orders when the Department of Justice (DOJ) issues a directive to restrict or block access to illegal computer data. Failure to do so can result in liability. Additionally, Section 5 states that intermediaries can be held liable if they "willfully aid or abet" in the commission of cybercrimes. However, liability is not automatic for all content transmitted through their platforms, indicating a limit to their responsibility.

Similarly, the Electronic Commerce Act of 2000 (Republic Act No. 8792), in Section 30, provides that intermediaries are not liable "merely for providing access to or for operating facilities for the transmission, routing, or storage of an electronic data message or electronic document." However, this immunity is conditional: intermediaries must not have knowledge of the illegal activity or content and must act to remove or block access when notified by authorities.

In conclusion, RA 8792 and RA 10175 contain some safe harbor provisions for intermediaries, these are limited in scope and effectiveness. The narrow definition of intermediaries and the specific content areas (electronic documents and cybercrimes) reduce the broader applicability of these laws in today’s internet landscape, leaving gaps in protection and practical enforcement, especially regarding intellectual property and other non-cybercrime issues. 

The Rules on Voluntary Administrative Site Blocking issued by the Intellectual Property Office of the Philippines (IPOPHL) also directly relate to safe harbor provisions for copyright infringements. The safe harbor provision is outlined in Section 11 of the IPOPHL Rules on Voluntary Administrative Site Blocking provides protection for Internet Service Providers (ISPs) regarding copyright infringement. This provision states that no administrative, criminal, or civil proceedings can be brought against ISPs for actions taken in good faith while performing their duties, provided that they do not have actual knowledge of the unlawful nature of the content they are hosting or transmitting. Furthermore, ISPs must not receive any financial benefit directly attributable to the unlawful activity. This framework effectively allows ISPs to operate without fear of liability for copyright infringement as long as they act responsibly and do not facilitate or knowingly engage in infringing activities​
