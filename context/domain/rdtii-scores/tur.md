### Indicator 2.1: Foreign exclusions from public procurement related to ICT goods and digital services
- **Score:** 1
- **Source Legislation:** The Public Procurement Law (PPL) No. 4734, Amended by Law No. 7257 of December 2020
- **Evidence/Impact:** The Public Procurement Law, specifically Article 63, allows contracting authorities to exclude foreign firms from public procurement under certain circumstances. These circumstances are tied to the estimated cost of the procurement. If the estimated cost falls below specific thresholds (detailed in Article 8), the contracting authority may exclude foreign bidders. This is not an absolute exclusion under all circumstances, but it does allow for exclusion based on a specific criterion (cost).

If the estimated cost of a tender is below the thresholds, the contracting authority may close the tender to foreign companies. For example:
- Goods and services procurement by central government units: 1,073,525 TL (approximately 300,000 USD).
- Goods and services procurement by other government units: 1,789,213 TL (approximately 500,000 USD).
- Works procurement: 39,362,920 TL (approximately 11 million USD).

If the estimated cost of a tender is equal to or above the thresholds, the contracting authority must open the tender to foreign companies. However, even in such cases, a price advantage of up to 15% may be applied in favor of domestic suppliers. Additionally, If a foreign company is part of a joint venture, the entire joint venture is considered foreign unless all partners are domestic.
- **Timeframe:** Since January 2003, last amended in December 2020

### Indicator 2.2: Specific requirements on source codes, encryption and trade secrets
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** The Public Procurement Law (PPL) No. 4734, Amended by Law No. 7257 of December 2020;

Public Procurement Contracts Law No. 4735 2002
- **Evidence/Impact:** There are no explicit requirements in Turkish public procurement law for tenderers to surrender patents, source codes, or trade secrets, there are provisions that protect the confidentiality of such information. The laws emphasize the importance of not disclosing proprietary information and ensuring that technical specifications do not unfairly restrict competition. Therefore, tenderers can generally expect that their proprietary information will be protected during the procurement process.

Public Procurement Law No. 4734:
- Article 29 of the Public Procurement Law No. 4734: "Persons in charge of implementing this Law and those who offer consulting services shall not disclose any confidential information or documents pertaining to contractor’s business or transactions or their technical or financial structures and shall not use them for their own or any third parties’ benefit."
- Article 12 of the Public Procurement Law No. 4734 requires that technical specifications must not include elements that impede competition. While this does not explicitly mention patents or trade secrets, it suggests that specifications should be designed to allow for fair competition without requiring proprietary information.
- Article 24 outlines the contents of procurement notices, which must include the criteria for participation and the required documents. While it does not specify the surrender of patents or trade secrets, it does require transparency in the procurement process.

Public Procurement Contracts Law No. 4735
- Article 29 of the Public Procurement Contracts Law No. 4735 states that persons in charge of implementing the law and those who offer consulting services must not disclose any confidential information or documents pertaining to a contractor’s business or transactions, including technical or financial structures. This implies a level of protection for proprietary information, including patents and trade secrets.

- Article 11 outlines the conditions under which individuals or entities may be prohibited from participating in tenders. While it does not explicitly mention local content, it establishes a framework for disqualification based on non-compliance with procurement regulations.

### Indicator 4.4: Lack of copyright framework and exceptions
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** The Law on Intellectual and Artistic Works Law No 5846 as amended by Law No. 5728
- **Evidence/Impact:** Türkiye has a comprehensive copyright framework established under the Law on Intellectual and Artistic Works (Law No. 5846), which has been amended multiple times to align with international standards and treaties. Türkiye's copyright law includes several exceptions and limitations to balance the rights of authors with public interest. These exceptions are consistent with international norms, such as those outlined in the Berne Convention and the TRIPS Agreement.

Articles 30–37 of Public Order and Public Interest:
- Works can be used as evidence in court or for public security purposes.
- Reproduction of laws, regulations, and court decisions is permitted.
- Speeches made in public assemblies or courts can be reproduced for news purposes.
- Educational and Research Use (Articles 33–34):
- Published works can be performed in educational institutions for face-to-face teaching without profit.
- Selected or collected works can be created for educational purposes, provided they do not conflict with the normal exploitation of the work.

Article 35 of Freedom of Quotation:
- Quotations from published works are allowed for purposes such as criticism, review, or scientific research, provided the source is cited.

Article 38 of Personal Use:
- Individuals can reproduce works for personal use without profit, as long as it does not conflict with the normal exploitation of the work or harm the rights holder's legitimate interests.

Article 40 - Use of Works in Public Spaces:
- Works of fine art permanently located in public spaces can be reproduced through photography or similar means.

Additional Article 11 of Special Provisions for Disabled Persons:
