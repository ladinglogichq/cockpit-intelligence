### Indicator 4.1: Patent application issues
- **Score:** 1

Patents (Amendment) Act (Act A1649) 2022
- **Evidence/Impact:** There are differential treatment relevant to patent applications in Malaysia. According to the Patents Act (Act 291) 1983, patent applicants who are not residents of Malaysia are explicitly required to appoint a local patent agent to act on their behalf. This is stipulated under the requirement for patent agents in Section 86(5), where non-residents must appoint local representatives to file and manage their applications.

Section 29A legal language stresses the need for all patent applications to comply with procedural and substantive examination requirements. If the applicant does not request a substantive or modified substantive examination within the required time period, the application is deemed withdrawn. If any discrimination occurred, it would likely be an issue of practice, not law.

Malaysia has a formal process for patent applications, including a preliminary and substantive examination. The transparency of the process is supported by the publication of patent applications in the Official Journal, as mandated under the Patents (Amendment) Act (Act A1649) 2022.

The Patents Act (Act 291) 1983 includes a requirement for residents of Malaysia to file patent applications domestically before filing abroad. Specifically, Section 23A of the Act mandates that residents must file a patent in Malaysia and wait two months before applying for a patent for the same invention abroad. This regulation ensures that the Malaysian government retains control over the disclosure of inventions before they are filed internationally.

### Indicator 4.2: Patent enforcement issues: civil and administrative procedures and remedies, provisional and other measures
- **Score:** 0
- **Source Legislation:** Patents Act (Act 291) 1983;

Patents (Amendment) Act (Act A1649) 2022
- **Evidence/Impact:** A patent owner has the right to initiate court proceedings (Section 59) against anyone who infringes or is likely to infringe the patent. This includes actions against actual infringement as well as “imminent infringement”, where acts make it likely that an infringement will occur.

Civil remedies such as injunctions (Section 60) and damages can be sought. The court may award damages to the patent owner and grant an injunction or  interim injunctions to prevent ongoing or imminent infringement and further infringement. Infringement proceedings must be initiated within five years of the infringing act to ensure timely action. Licensees and compulsory license beneficiaries also have the right to initiate infringement proceedings (Section 61), provided certain conditions are met.

The Registrar of Patents can grant a compulsory license (Section 51) if the patent has not been adequately exploited in Malaysia (Section 49). After three years from the patent grant, or four years from the filing date, a third party may apply for a compulsory license under certain circumstances, such as insufficient production or unreasonable pricing. The process of obtaining a compulsory license involves proving the absence of legitimate reasons for not working the patent, which may present a high burden for applicants.

Enforcement officers (Section 70) authorized by the Ministry have the power to conduct searches and seizures as part of patent enforcement efforts. This includes the ability to seize goods that are suspected of infringing on patents. In Section 69 Officers may be authorized to arrest individuals involved in patent infringement if necessary, demonstrating Malaysia’s robust enforcement mechanisms for intellectual property rights.
