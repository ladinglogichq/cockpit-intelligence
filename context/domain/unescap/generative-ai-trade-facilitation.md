# Generative AI for trade facilitation and control: Prefer integration to revolution

### 

![Aerial view container ship cargo freight shipping international, Global business freight shipping import export logistic transportation container ship](/sites/default/d8files/styles/thumbnail/public/2025-09/AdobeStock_1630237030_2025-M09-08.jpeg?itok=H2w2Oa3z)

Photo credit: Adobe Stock Photo/Kalyakan

Photo credit: Adobe Stock Photo/Kalyakan

AI is the new hype, replacing blockchain as the most “in-demand” technology solution in logistics and customs.

Unlike blockchain or distributed ledger technology, however, AI covers a broad range of technologies and has been used widely in customs and logistics for over a decade. Predictive AI systems, based on structured data and rules or deep learning techniques with unstructured data, have long been used to determine the best routes and analyze x-ray scans of containers. What is new here, and what people now refer to as AI, is generative AI, most commonly large language models (LLMs) used to generate new content. These models excel at processing human language and respond in excellent English – or most other languages, making them sound very intelligent indeed. They also “know everything”, able to provide a well-formulated answer to almost any question.

However, in a recent [webinar on AI for cross-border paperless trade](https://www.unescap.org/events/2025/webinar-ai-agents-and-bots-facilitating-paperless-trade-transactions), speakers from German Customs explained that while they were pilot testing generative AI for their online chatbot interacting with stakeholders, they were pleased with the current “legacy” chatbot they had and had no immediate plans to replace it. A key reason for this was that this rules-based chatbot was fully immune to the possibility of hallucinations. In contrast, it remains very difficult to fully control generative AI chatbots and solutions outputs, even when using retrieval augmented generation (RAG) and fine-tuning of the underlying general purpose AI models.

As discussed on the sidelines of the [APEC Customs Business Dialogue](https://mddb.apec.org/Documents/2025/SCCP/SCCP2/25_sccp2_019.pdf) in Incheon with service providers developing e-government solutions for trade facilitation, many agencies and officials now want AI solutions you can talk (write) to and get answers to everything they ask. Risk management systems that would flag potentially high-risk shipments are not enough. Users would like a risk management system that can explain in human language why a particular shipment has been flagged and be able to chat with them about any follow-up queries they may have. This was also the main request when we presented the first version of the Cambodia [Trade Intelligence and Negotiation Advisor](https://tina.trade) (TINA): Could we just type in the trade partner we have in mind and TINA would do all the required calculations and analysis and return us the answer, ideally in the form of a well-formatted report?

This would be a really nice-to-have, indeed. But is this really a must-have? How much of the project budget should be spent developing this human-like “intelligent” interface, as opposed to focusing on making sure the system always flags the correct shipments for inspection in the first place or that the development and analysis of alternative trade negotiation scenarios follow well-specified criteria? These questions are particularly important for developing countries with limited resources and capacity to maintain these systems.

A recent [IMF technical note](https://www.imf.org/en/Publications/TNM/Issues/2025/08/09/Generative-Artificial-Intelligence-for-Compliance-Risk-Analysis-Applications-in-Tax-and-567429?cid=em-COM-789-50544) provides an insightful and promising view of the current use of generative AI in tax and customs compliance applications. At the same time, according to the MIT’s Media Lab, 95 per cent of corporate generative AI initiatives showed zero returns and only 5 per cent made it to the final production with measurable value. The [MIT report](https://mlq.ai/media/quarterly_decks/v0.1_State_of_AI_in_Business_2025_Report.pdf) reveals that real returns of AI initiatives so far have come from less glamorous areas: back-office automation, procurement, finance and operations.

This brings us back to “old AI”, machine learning and predictive models. Ensuring the models are trained on sound data and are fed high-quality data is most important and should not be compromised in any way. The “garbage in, garbage out” principle in data analytics continues to apply and the lack of transparency on the datasets used to train and develop today’s generative AI engines means that what went “into” these models remains unknown. Although unproven generative AI outputs may be more easily understood by untrained personnel, they should be approached with great caution at this stage.

Integrating generative AI and predictive systems may prove particularly beneficial but come with challenges. Technical integration can be complex, with potentially different data pipelines to be established to feed both types of models and slower overall response time. Feedback loops where generative AI responses guided by predictive outputs influence future predictions can amplify errors or biases. Finally, Gen AI are computationally expensive to train and run, adding significantly to cost and resource demands. At present, the most feasible approach may be a superficial integration at the end-user interface level, using generative AI primarily to present the results of the underlying predictive model in natural language.

In conclusion, the following recommendations can be made at this time: First, be very careful when considering general-purpose AI engines as a foundation for critical e-government systems. Second, prioritize the development of advanced predictive AI solutions and underlying datasets which can be validated and controlled. Third, if there are sufficient resources and capacity, consider integrating predictive AI with generative AI, but focus initially on end-user interface-level integration, ensuring continuous supervision by skilled personnel to mitigate errors, biases, or misinterpretation. Finally, given the great potential for AI in accelerating trade digitalization, interested countries may consider setting up a dedicated intergovernmental working group to share experiences and develop common guidelines in this area under the [Framework Agreement on Facilitation of Cross-Border Paperless Trade in Asia and the Pacific](https://www.unescap.org/projects/cpta).
